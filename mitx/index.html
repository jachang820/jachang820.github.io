Journey in rigorous statistics.

MITx’s MicroMasters in Statistics and Data Science confers no university credit, but is accepted as a replacement of the course core requirements of their PhD program Social and Engineering Systems (SES). The certificate is achieved by completion of four courses followed by a comprehensive proctored exam, all of which share the same level of rigor as the residential versions at MIT. This is explained in their program letter.

Together with a couple other courses I’ve taken before, the Micromasters is also equivalent to most of the core requirements of MIT’s Minor in Statistics and Data Science.

Coursework

Probability - The Science of Uncertainty and Data

Rigorously establishes the fundamentals from probability to measure to the central limit theorem (CLT), and then to statistical processes and Markov chains. This course is valuable for describing not only math, but intuition. It ends in interpreting the wide applicability of Bernoulli and Poisson processes, and how they could be used with the CLT. This justifies statistical theory for most observable processes.

Fundamentals of Statistics

The highlight of the program, this course rigorously establishes the theoretical connection between the central limit theorem and the foundations of inference: estimation, hypothesis testing and confidence intervals. Most importantly, it goes deep enough into the math to convey the assumptions and conditions that make everything work, and when they fall apart. It discusses some pitfalls in the p-value, and how to account for multiple hypothesis testing. It ends in demonstrating that the aforementioned Bernoulli and Poisson processes fall into the family of the generalized linear model (GLM) through the canonical link function. This was mindblowing for me since it justifies the simplicity of the math behind machine learning algorithms like regression, and that the assumptions are valid.

Data Analysis in Social Science (Elective)

This course provides some details in randomized controlled trial (RCT) design, dealing with confounding variables, including some case studies. It also describes how to interpret linear models and demonstrate causality.

Machine Learning with Python - From Linear Models to Deep Learning

This is a typical machine learning first or second course, but goes a little deeper into mathematical theory. It covers linear models, support vector machines, feed-forward neural networks, recurrent neural networks, times series analysis, and reinforcement learning.

Data Analysis: Statistical Modeling and Computation in Applications

This is a case study, project-based capstone course that covers five domains in statistical modeling. Please see my writings for more details.
