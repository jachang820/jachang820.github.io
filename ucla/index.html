JONATHAN CHANG

Learning to learn.

UCLA taught me a lot, but most importantly it gave me the tools and confidence to research what I wanted to learn. That there is no magic authority that grants or disseminate knowledge. They could all be found in textbooks and other references.

Within electrical engineering, I had found that signals was the most interesting subject. The techniques used there are heavily rooted in mathematical and statistical theory, with modulation schemes and noise rejection. It really opened my eyes when I realized how many domains this could be applicable to. It wasn’t necessarily radio signals that I loved, but the idea that these mathematical concepts had a direct application. That math, and by extension computer science, is a formalization of the problems that I wanted to solve.

Our engineering major had a technical breadth requirement of three courses in an area complementary to electrical engineering. Once I discovered my passion in software, I took more than the requisite number. For topics that I couldn’t take at UCLA, I looked online to massively online open courseware (MOOCs). Together with my technical breadth courses, I credit a couple online courses toward helping me be able to contribute to my eventual team, Wild Cardinals, that would win the top prize at the university data science hackathon, DataFest 2019.

Operating Systems

The assignments from this course consists of some programs that perform Linux client and server operation, atomic operations on list sorting, simplistic file system analysis, and setting up an IoT server that monitors and reports temperature. These are written primarily in C, and some Ruby.

Client and Server, Multithreading, and IoT (Github)

Data Mining

A Kaggle competition was set up for the class for the prediction of Yelp scores based on reviews and various other features. The following project was mostly written in R.

Prediction of Yelp Reviews (Github)

Robotics

The assignment topic revolved around robot communication and kinematic linkages. The first project was intended to control musical instruments using an online interface. The second project was intended to move a robot using an online interface. However, for the second project, only a front-end simulation with controls was produced. Both projects were written with Javascript and Python (Django), and C++ on the Arduino side.

Instrument Playing Robot (Github)
